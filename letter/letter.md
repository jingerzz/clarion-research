## Q2: April — June

**Updated: 2026-05-13**

### Regime & Environment

The system entered the quarter in ORANGE — the same late-cycle concentrated leadership it's flagged since early May. The S&P 500 sits near all-time highs, but breadth tells a different story: the equal-weight RSP index has lagged the cap-weight SPY by 7.9% over 60 days. That's the widest spread since late 2021. Translation: a handful of mega-caps are carrying the tape while the average stock is struggling. This is not a "risk-on" market, no matter what the headline index suggests.

The CAPE ratio sits at 42.05 — only two periods in history have been more expensive (1999 and 2021). Both ended poorly for buy-and-hold equity investors over the subsequent decade. The system computes an equity hurdle of 9.69% (3.69% risk-free rate + 6.0% ORANGE regime premium). That's a high bar — and intentionally so. It means every dollar we put into equities has to clear not just the T-bill rate but a meaningful premium for the regime risk we're taking.

The dominant narrative right now is AI infrastructure spending. NVDA, MSFT, META, and GOOGL are guiding capex higher quarter after quarter. The market has rewarded the semiconductor and cloud names relentlessly — and punished almost everything else. This concentration is both the opportunity and the risk: if the AI capex cycle continues accelerating, the regime goes GREEN and we want exposure. If it pauses, the unwind could be sharp and the "boring" value names we're accumulating — IBM at 16x, BBY at 8x, DECK at 14x — become the safe harbor.

Two events this quarter directly shaped our portfolio: Goldman Sachs double-downgraded BBY in late April, sending it to $55 and giving us a 52-week-low entry on a 6.7% yielder. And IREN closed a $2.6B convertible note in May — validating its balance sheet and definitively clearing a kill condition (cash below $500M). Both were gifts: Goldman created an entry on a stock we already liked; NVIDIA's IREN contract proved the thesis while the stock was still digesting the convert overhang.

The tariff environment remains uncertain — and that matters for DECK (Vietnam/Cambodia supply chain) and IBM (global hardware manufacturing). We're watching both. Windows 10 end-of-life already passed (October 2025), and the enterprise AI PC upgrade cycle is starting to show up in PC shipment data (+3.2% Q1 YoY). BBY is the biggest physical beneficiary of that wave — if it's real.

### What We Learned

1. **ORANGE regime is psychologically harder than GREEN or RED.** In GREEN, you buy. In RED, you hide. In ORANGE, the correct action is almost always "size smaller" or "wait" — and that feels wrong when you've just done the research, found cheap names, and have cash earning 4%. The 83% T-bill allocation is deliberate resistance against the pressure to deploy. The five GTC limit orders sitting below current prices are the visible expression of this discipline. Patience in ORANGE is not inaction — it's positioning.

2. **The screener score and the thesis health score measure completely different things.** IBM scored 34.4 on the 8-factor composite — rank #11, a "pass" that almost got ignored. But the thesis health framework gives it ~61 based on valuation safety (+35% to base case) and business health (governance moat intact). The screener can't see through book-value accounting artifacts — IBM's decades of buybacks crushed book equity to near zero, so D/E shows 211x when the actual debt/EBITDA is 4.2x. A screener is a filter, not a verdict. The thesis is the verdict.

3. **Insider buying at scale is the most underweighted signal in quantitative screens.** Jeff Green's $148M personal TTD purchase at $24.71 — 6 million shares, open market, no option exercise, no RSU vesting — is the largest insider buy in the portfolio by orders of magnitude. No screener factor captures it. No yfinance field surfaces it. It came from reading an external thesis. The stock is now 17% below his cost basis. If the CEO controlling 50% of voting power puts $148M of his own cash at risk, that is more valuable information than any P/E ratio. We need a systematic way to surface insider signals.

4. **The discipline of not buying is as important as the discipline of buying.** Five names passed the value screen. Five GTC limit orders were set below current prices. All five are still open. FFIV at $357 vs $325 limit — watched it run from $345. MSFT at $405 vs $400 add zone — could have bought at $402 intraday and didn't. In a bull market, these non-decisions feel like mistakes. In a late-cycle ORANGE regime with CAPE at 42, they're the decisions that preserve capital for the gifts that actually arrive — like BBY at $55 and TTD at $20.

5. **External research is a force multiplier.** The TTD, BBY, and HOOD theses all originated from the external repository at clarion-theses-jing.zocomputer.io — not from the Clarion screener pipeline. The system is excellent at verifying and deepening existing ideas (SEC filings → evidence, regime → sizing, yfinance → quality snapshots). But idea generation — the spark that says "this is interesting, look closer" — still benefits from diverse sources. Building a regular scan of external research into the weekly process would compound over time.
