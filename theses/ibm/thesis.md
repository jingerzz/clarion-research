## Core Thesis

### What I Believe

IBM is the contrarian AI play for regulated industries. The thesis is not about AI hype — it's about AI governance and hybrid cloud necessity in the sectors that cannot move fast without compliance guardrails. Financial services, healthcare, defense, and government together represent trillions of dollars of IT spend. These sectors cannot simply migrate to OpenAI APIs or Azure OpenAI due to data sovereignty requirements, model explainability obligations (EU AI Act, US Executive Order on AI), and audit trail requirements. IBM's Watsonx.ai + Watsonx.governance suite directly targets this gap. Red Hat (acquired 2019, now a $14B+ revenue contributor) provides the hybrid cloud orchestration layer that lets regulated enterprises run AI in their own data centers. HashiCorp (acquired 2024 for $6.4B) adds infrastructure automation and IaC (Terraform) that is already the enterprise standard for cloud provisioning.

At 20.33x trailing P/E (17.08x forward), IBM is the cheapest name in this AI screen by a wide margin. The high stated D/E ratio (211x) is an artifact of aggressive share buybacks reducing book equity to near-zero — IBM's actual debt/EBITDA is approximately 3-4x, which is manageable. FCF TTM of $13.08B is real cash generation. ROE of 35.77% reflects the quality of the underlying business, not leverage inflation (ROA 5.37% confirms the leverage is not the entire story).

The AI governance regulatory wave is arriving on a defined schedule: EU AI Act enforcement ramps 2025-2026, US federal procurement AI requirements are expanding, and regulated industries are increasingly required to document AI decision pathways. IBM is the only large enterprise software company that specifically sells AI governance as a product (Watsonx.governance). This is a 2-3 year patience thesis — the governance wave lags the model wave by 18-24 months, and IBM is positioned for that lag.

The risk is that the legacy consulting and mainframe businesses continue to shrink faster than AI/Red Hat/HashiCorp grows. That is the thesis-monitor question: can new IBM grow faster than old IBM declines? Current evidence says yes — management's stated focus is hybrid cloud + AI, and the 2025 annual report reflects organic + inorganic investment in exactly those areas.

### Why I Believe It (Evidence)

**Moat & competitive position**

1. IBM's strategic differentiation is explicitly in "higher-value capabilities, including security, scalability, and reliability" — not price competition. The 10-K describes the moat as capabilities that regulated industries require and that commodity cloud providers do not offer at the same compliance certification level. Source: IBM 10-K filed 2026-02-24 → business

2. IBM "proactively partners with a broad variety of companies including hyperscalers" — meaning IBM and AWS/Azure/GCP are complementary, not purely competitive. IBM runs on top of cloud, not against it. This is the Red Hat hybrid cloud strategy: run RHEL and OpenShift on any cloud, on any on-prem infrastructure, with a unified control plane. Source: IBM 10-K filed 2026-02-24 → business

3. The IBM 10-K explicitly calls out "AI development, deployment, governance" as a product investment category in the risk factors — by naming AI governance as a risk area requiring investment, IBM is confirming it is a revenue-generating activity material enough to disclose. Source: IBM 10-K filed 2026-02-24 → risk_factors/chunk1

**Management & capital allocation**

4. IBM's stated strategy is "optimize our portfolio with organic and inorganic innovations and effective resource allocation" — the HashiCorp and Red Hat acquisitions are examples of this. Management has a track record of buying enterprise infrastructure assets that take 3-5 years to integrate and compound. Source: IBM 10-K filed 2026-02-24 → business

5. FCF TTM $13.08B is converted at a high rate from operating CF of $13.99B — working capital management is disciplined, minimal drag between earnings and cash. The dividend is sustainable and has been growing for 28+ consecutive years.

**Financial trends**

6. The financial statements and MD&A reference the IBM 2025 Annual Report to Stockholders by incorporation — the 10-K is a thin wrapper. The actual financials require pulling the Annual Report for deeper trend analysis. ROE 35.77%, FCF $13.08B, op margin 13.81% are the key available metrics. Source: IBM 10-K filed 2026-02-24 → mdna, financial_statements

7. Operating margin 13.81% is the lowest of the five names in this screen — this is the cost of IBM's hybrid model (hardware, services, software, consulting all bundled). As the mix shifts toward higher-margin software (Red Hat, Watsonx, HashiCorp), margins should expand. This is the long-term margin expansion thesis.

**Risk factors**

8. IBM is exposed to financial services sector dynamics as its largest vertical — if FS companies face liquidity or credit stress, IBM IT spending contracts. The 10-K explicitly names financial services as a key customer industry risk. Source: IBM 10-K filed 2026-02-24 → risk_factors/chunk3

9. Tariff and trade restriction risk is real: IBM manufactures hardware (mainframes, Power servers, storage) in global supply chains. Source: IBM 10-K filed 2026-02-24 → risk_factors/chunk0

### Why Now (Catalyst / Patience Rationale)

This is the most explicitly patience-based of the five theses. The AI governance regulatory wave (EU AI Act, US federal AI requirements) is arriving on a predictable 12-24 month timeline. IBM is the only enterprise company with a purpose-built AI governance product line. The market does not price this: IBM trades at 17x forward, similar to a mature slow-growth company, when the governance software TAM is beginning to grow at 30%+. The catalyst is when Watsonx.governance revenue becomes a disclosed segment or shows up in analyst models. Until then, patience and the 2.9% dividend yield while you wait.
