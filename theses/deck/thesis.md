## Core Thesis

### What I Believe

Deckers owns two of the most recognizable footwear brands in the world — UGG and HOKA — and trades at 14.3× trailing earnings with 57.9% gross margins and 40% ROE. The market is pricing DECK like a seasonal fashion company. The data says it's a margin-expanding compounder.

The insight the market is missing: DECK is systematically cheap because UGG carries a fashion-company discount. But HOKA's performance/athletic positioning makes the combined franchise far more durable than that framing implies. HOKA grew +23.6% to $2.23B in FY2025 and is on pace to surpass UGG as the primary revenue driver within 2-3 years. When HOKA (a year-round athletic brand) crosses that threshold, it becomes very difficult for the market to maintain the "seasonal fashion" narrative.

Three consecutive years of gross margin expansion — 50.3% → 55.6% → 57.9% — is not an accounting artifact. It is evidence that DECK is selling into its brand equity, not competing on price. That is the moat. Management is also demonstrating capital discipline: Sanuk divested, Koolaburra wound down, active buybacks at 40% ROE. The business earns its way to a higher multiple; the market just hasn't recognized it yet.

### Why Now (Catalyst / Patience Rationale)

No single near-term catalyst. Patience play. Each quarterly print where HOKA shows sustained >15% growth and margins hold is a data point that chips away at the fashion-brand discount. Two additional factors: (1) the stock is 23% off its 52-week high ($133.43 → $102.72) — a fair entry, not a gift; (2) tariff clarity around Vietnam/Cambodia supply chain exposure could be a positive surprise vs. current fear pricing. The re-rating event is when HOKA crosses UGG in revenue — that is likely FY2027-28.
